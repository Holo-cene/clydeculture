## Brand & Tone of Voice — Working Guide

*For anyone writing for Clyde Culture: copy, listings, social, print, or editorial.*

---

## What Clyde Culture Is

Clyde Culture is Glasgow's shared noticeboard for culture. It makes what's happening
across the city visible and easy to find — music, theatre, workshops, exhibitions,
community events, club nights, zine fairs, opera, DIY gigs. Independent and established.
Free and ticketed.

It does not rank. It does not recommend. It does not decide what matters. It makes
everything findable, and leaves the rest to Glasgow.

It's run as a community collective. Membership revenue goes towards the platform and
helps fund local events. Members decide how that funding is allocated, and annual reports
are published publicly.

If it's happening here, it belongs here.

---

## The Emotional Core

Clyde Culture exists so Glasgow can see itself.

Every editorial decision passes through this. Does this help the city see itself clearly
and fairly? If not, it doesn't belong here.

---

## Brand Personality

**Primary: Cultural Infrastructure.** Stable, useful, clear, reliable. The kind of thing
that should have always existed.

**Secondary: Careful Organiser.** Things are in the right place. Categories make sense.
Nothing feels random or accidental.

**Undercurrent: Independent.** The independence shows in how the site works — not in how
it talks about itself. A free event listed at the same visual weight as a ticketed one.
An easy, unpaid submission process. Public funding reports. These are values made
structural. They don't need announcing.

---

## The Four Principles

**Clear.** Say what something is. Concrete nouns, direct verbs, specific information. No
hype, no promotional adjectives, no abstract language. Clarity is warmth — it respects
the reader's time.

> *An exhibition of new work by five Glasgow-based artists.*

Not: *An unmissable celebration of Glasgow's vibrant creative landscape.*

**Steady.** Calm and confident, not loud or stiff. A steady tone builds trust. It isn't
cold — it has personality — but it doesn't perform enthusiasm it doesn't mean.

> *New exhibitions this week.*
> *Live music at Mono on Friday.*
> *Free workshop in Govanhill.*

The difference between steady and lifeless is specificity. When copy feels flat, add
detail — not excitement.

**Useful.** Every word helps someone understand what, where, when, and how to attend.
Navigation is plain. Buttons are direct. Labels are consistent and never change between
pages. Clear over clever, always.

**Open.** Language doesn't create hierarchy between events. Nothing is cutting-edge,
prestigious, emerging, or unmissable — these words rank things, and this site doesn't. A
zine fair sits beside an opera. A club night sits beside a hiking group. The structure
expresses the values.

---

## The Glaswegian Tone

The rhythm is Scottish. The spelling is not.

The voice is direct and unhurried, with a preference for concrete words over abstract
ones. It sounds like a well-written programme note from a Glasgow venue — considered and
specific. It sounds local without announcing it.

Dialect spelling, forced localisms, and dropped-in "wee"s for warmth all do the opposite
of what they intend. The rhythm of plain, direct Scottish English is already warm.

---

## Energy Levels

Not all copy runs at the same temperature. Most does — but some moments need a slight
lift.

**Default (90% of copy).** Steady, neutral, informative. The examples above.

**Launches and milestones.** Still restrained, but slightly more expressive. No
exclamation marks required.

> *Clyde Culture launches this week. Everything happening across Glasgow, in one place.*

**Funding announcements.** Factual and transparent, with measured warmth.

> *Members have allocated £2,000 to four community events this quarter. Full report
> available here.*

The lift comes from what is being said, not from how hard it's being said.

---

## Accessibility

Accessibility is a value, not a feature. A site that works clearly for everyone —
including people who are dyslexic, neurodivergent, older, or new to Glasgow — is making a
statement about who culture is for.

Short sentences. One idea per sentence. No rhetorical questions. No sarcasm. No dense
metaphor. Consistent labels that don't change between pages. All images have descriptive
alt text, written with the same care as any other copy.

Before publishing anything, ask: would someone new to Glasgow understand this? Would
someone scanning quickly understand this? Would someone using assistive technology
understand this? If not, rewrite it.

---

## What It Must Never Sound Like

Clyde Culture must never sound exclusive, in-group coded, corporate, or like a council
statement. It must never perform coolness, perform virtue, or perform authority. It must
never be preachy, trend-led, politically combative, youth-chasing, or tourist-facing. It
must never be generic.

The test: could this have been written by a tourism board, a startup, or a council
communications team? If yes, start again.

## Writing for Different Contexts

### Event Listings

Neutral and complete. The listing says what something is, where it is, and when it is. It
doesn't say whether it's good.

> **Screenprinting Workshop**
> Govanhill Baths · Saturday · 2–4pm
> A beginner-friendly workshop on screenprinting. All materials provided.

> **Live Music**
> Mono · Friday · 8pm
> Four Glasgow-based acts. Pay what you can on the door.

If a community group submits a listing with their own voice — warmer, more enthusiastic —
preserve it. Lightly edit for clarity and accuracy, but don't smooth out what makes it
theirs. House style applies to editorial and navigational copy, not to every individual
listing.

If an event description contains political language, it stays. That is the event's voice.
Clyde Culture ensures clarity and factual accuracy — it doesn't rewrite copy to
neutralise it. Clyde Culture's neutrality is about its own editorial voice, not about
sanitising the city.

> Become a member
> Support Clyde Culture

> Membership contributions fund the platform and support local cultural events. Members

### Social Media

Short, clear, aligned with the site. The events do the work.

> *This week on Clyde Culture: theatre, live music, workshops, and three free events
> across the city.*

> *Free film screening tonight in Partick. 7pm.*

No cultural commentary, no engagement bait, no platform-specific personality shift. Point
back to the site.

### Print and Poster

> What's on in Glasgow

> clydeculture.com

Design does the heavy lifting. Copy gets out of the way.

---

### Editorial Features

If short features or highlights are published, they stay fact-led and neutral in
evaluation. A feature about a DIY gig carries the same weight as one about a touring
production. Community voices are quoted directly and in full. If a writer is brought in,
their work runs under their name in their own voice — it isn't absorbed into house style.

Editorial highlights. It doesn't critique.

---

## The Political Neutrality Principle

Structural neutrality is not moral silence. Clyde Culture does not editorialise, rank, or
preach — but it is not indifferent. The values are visible in what is included, how things
are presented, how funding is distributed, and how accessible the site is.

Clyde Culture does not comment publicly on political events or news stories. If pressed,
the honest answer is: Clyde Culture lists everything fairly and supports community-led
decisions about funding. What matters in Glasgow's cultural life is for Glasgow to decide.

---

## Edge Cases

**A venue or organisation has a contested reputation.** Clyde Culture lists events. It
doesn't endorse organisations or comment on their histories. If something is part of
Glasgow's cultural life, it belongs here.

**A submitted listing doesn't match house style.** Edit lightly for clarity and accuracy.
Preserve the contributor's voice. See listings guidance above.

**Someone asks Clyde Culture to take a position on a news story.** It doesn't. See the
political neutrality principle above.
