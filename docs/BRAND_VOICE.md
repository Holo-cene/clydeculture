# Clyde Culture — Brand Voice

This is the working reference for anyone writing Clyde Culture copy. The source document
is `docs/reference/DESIGN_LANGUAGE.md`. Where the two disagree, the source document wins.

---

## Where this applies

House style governs **editorial and navigational copy**: headings, section labels, calls
to action, homepage text, about pages, social posts, alt text, print materials, and any
words written by Clyde Culture itself.

It does **not** govern individual event listings submitted by community groups. A listing
submitted with warmth, enthusiasm, or political language should be lightly edited for
clarity and factual accuracy, not smoothed into house style. The contributor's voice stays.
Clyde Culture's neutrality belongs to Clyde Culture — it is not imposed on the city it lists.

---

## The four principles

**Clear.** Say what something is. Concrete nouns, direct verbs, specific information.
No hype, no promotional adjectives. Clarity is warmth — it respects the reader's time.

> *An exhibition of new work by five Glasgow-based artists.*
> Not: *An unmissable celebration of Glasgow's vibrant creative landscape.*

**Steady.** Calm and confident, not loud or stiff. Personality comes from specificity,
not from performed enthusiasm. When copy feels flat, add detail — not excitement.

**Useful.** Every word helps someone understand what, where, when, and how to attend.
Navigation is plain. Buttons are direct. Labels are consistent and don't change between pages.
Clear over clever, always.

**Open.** Nothing is cutting-edge, emerging, prestigious, or unmissable. These words rank
events, and this platform does not. A zine fair sits beside an opera. A club night sits
beside a hiking group. The structure expresses the values.

## The Glaswegian tone

The rhythm is Scottish. The spelling is not.

Direct, unhurried, plain. It sounds like a well-written programme note from a Glasgow
venue — considered and specific, local without announcing it. Dialect spelling, forced
localisms, and dropped-in "wee"s for warmth all do the opposite of what they intend.
Plain, direct Scottish English is already warm.

---

## Never rank

Do not use language that implies an event is better than, more important than, or more
worth attending than another. This includes words like: unmissable, cutting-edge, emerging,
prestigious, vibrant, exciting, unique, or world-class.

The test: if a free zine fair couldn't carry this word without irony, the word doesn't belong.

---

## Accessibility

Short sentences. One idea per sentence. No rhetorical questions. No sarcasm. No dense
metaphor. Consistent labels that don't shift between pages.

All images have descriptive alt text, written with the same care as any other copy. The
alt text says what is shown — it does not editorialize.

Before publishing: would someone new to Glasgow understand this? Would someone scanning
quickly understand this? Would someone using assistive technology understand this?
If not, rewrite it.

---

## What it must never sound like

Clyde Culture must never sound exclusive, in-group coded, corporate, or like a council
statement. It must never perform coolness, perform virtue, or perform authority. It must
never be preachy, trend-led, or tourist-facing.

The test: could this have been written by a tourism board, a startup, or a council
communications team? If yes, start again.

---

## Social media

Short, clear, aligned with the site. The events do the work.

> *This week on Clyde Culture: theatre, live music, workshops, and three free events across
> the city.*

> *Free film screening tonight in Partick. 7pm.*

No cultural commentary, no engagement bait, no platform-specific personality shift. Point
back to the site.

---

## Calls to action

> Submit your event · Become a member · Support Clyde Culture · View all events · Read more

Never: *Join the movement. Be part of something. Let's build together. Discover more.*
